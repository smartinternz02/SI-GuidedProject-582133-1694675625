  2. Survival and Fare:

    • There is a significant positive correlation (0.257307) between survival and fare. This implies that passengers who paid higher fares were more likely to survive. It reinforces the observation made regarding passenger class, as higher-class tickets generally cost more.

  3. Age and Passenger Class (Pclass):

    • There is a significant negative correlation (-0.331339) between passenger age and class (Pclass). This indicates that older passengers tended to be in lower classes, which is not surprising as younger passengers might be more inclined to travel in higher classes.

  4. Siblings/Spouse (SibSp) and Parents/Children (Parch):

    • There is a significant positive correlation (0.414838) between the number of siblings/spouse (SibSp) and the number of parents/children (Parch) a passenger had. This suggests that passengers who had more siblings/spouse were also more likely to have more parents/children onboard.

  5. Fare and Passenger Class (Pclass):

    • There is a significant negative correlation (-0.549500) between fare and passenger class (Pclass). This reaffirms the relationship between class and fare, indicating that higher-class tickets are associated with higher fares.

  6. Survival and Siblings/Spouse (SibSp) / Parents/Children (Parch):

    • There are relatively weak correlations between survival and the number of siblings/spouse (SibSp) and parents/children (Parch). However, they are worth considering, as they suggest that passengers with more family members onboard (either siblings/spouse or parents/children) may have had a slightly lower chance of survival. This might indicate that larger families faced more difficulties during the disaster.
